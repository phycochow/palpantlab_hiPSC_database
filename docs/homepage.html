Data Server for >300 Published hiPSC-CM Maturation and Differentiation Protocols

Over the years, researchers have developed 2D and 3D protocols to promote the cardiac differentiation and maturation of human stem cells (hiPSC-CMs). However, there has been a lack of method benchmarking and knowledge consolidation as the methods are often exclusive to a few research groups and the results are not directly comparable. Our study addresses these gaps by augmenting a robust feature selection approach for protocol optimisation. Through comprehensive statistical analyses and functional validation studies, we find common themes that influence the physiological performance of hiPSC-CMs.

About this Server

What analyses were done?

We performed data mining on 314 hiPSC-CM studies to make data-driven recommendations to enhance the physiological maturation of hiPSC-derived cardiomyocytes. For each maturation parameters, we established 15 series of protocol combinations, like the cell's genetic background, that are enriched to impact downstream hiPSC-CM function. Then, we used the database to guide in vitro validation, demonstrating the roles of enriched media and supplements in promoting the functional maturation of hiPSC-CM.

Our preprint is publicly available at https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2024.09.04.611313v1.

What does the current dataset contain?

Ewoldt et al. systematically analysed 300 studies focusing on maturation benchmarking for ventricular cardiomyocytes, which aims to improve hiPSC-CMs’ embryonic characteristics for the suitability of modeling adult cardiac diseases. In section V, the authors determined that most protocols are exclusive to research groups and not appropriately compared to other existing ones. The authors conclude there is insufficient data to identify protocol parameters that yield the best functional outcomes, which in turn hinders the progression of the field. They provide the community with a dataset with 56 (study characteristics) x 300 (hiPSC studies) and urge researchers to continue adding to the starting database.

In our ongoing study, we compiled systematic evidence that glycaemic variability in diabetes is a significant risk factor for heart muscle dysfunction and ischemia. We included (a) 14 metabolic maturation studies to Ewoldt et al.’s dataset, (b) annotated all 314 studies with cell sex and ancestry, and performed a quantitative analysis on hiPSC-CM functional outcomes to enrich for protocol parameters.

What are the limitations?

One of the key contributions of this study is its ability to overcome the near-incomparability of context-dependent hiPSC-CM protocols, revealing design-outcome associations. Traditionally, wet-lab protocols are tailored to specific research questions, making direct comparisons challenging. From a dry-lab perspective, missing information that cannot be imputed, such as the use of certain maturation factors or performance measurements, further complicates comparisons. For example, a limited set of 48 studies measuring contractile force values lacks the statistical power for meaningful predictions.

Our rigorous quantitative analysis significantly improves feasibility and provides confidence values for statistical assessments. We acknowledge that these results are subject to change based on the following limitations and encourage users to upload their own protocols for comparison against similar studies.

  • Ascertainment Bias: While we have shown the dissimilarity of enrichments, they are based on the most reproduced study designs. Emerging modifications that could improve protocols might not be identified due to limited representation.
  • Generalisation Challenges: It is difficult to generalize the biological causality of molecular variables towards functional outcomes, as some may counteract others that inhibit maturation.
  • Ordinal Relationships: Tree models paired with one-hot encoding do not retain ordinal relationships between categories, which means the original study characteristics might be used in the same path, potentially making the enrichment a technical artifact. Fortunately, our novel bootstrapping-based technique assigns statistical insignificance.
  • Assay Design Differences: The database may overlook critical differences between assay designs, such as the cellular complexity and nutrient requirements of 2D versus 3D culture models, leading to contradictions in enrichment for maturation and modeling.

FAQ

How do I commit my experimental data?

We encourage all users to upload and QC existing data regardless of whether it is published. On this server, we offer a similarity check through the Study Database to find similar studies based on protocol variables that users define. This aims to help with protocol benchmarking and estimating the impact of variable changes in user's protocol designs. By filling in the fields, we will recieve an email with your uploaded protocol, which we will manually process and integrate it into the exisiting database.
